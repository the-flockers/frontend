# The Dangers of Automated License Plate Recognition (ALPR)

## What is ALPR?

Automated License Plate Recognition (ALPR) technology uses optical character recognition (OCR) to read vehicle license plates from images or video feeds. Systems like Flock Safety have deployed thousands of cameras across U.S. municipalities, creating a pervasive surveillance infrastructure that captures and logs every vehicle's movements.

## The Core Threats

### 1. **Mass Surveillance Without Consent**

ALPR systems do not target suspects. They photograph and record every vehicle in range regardless of whether the driver has committed any crime. Residents of surveilled neighborhoods are automatically logged into government databases simply for driving on public roads.

- **Scale**: Flock operates across 5,000+ camera networks across the U.S.
- **Coverage**: A single commute can generate dozens of timestamped location records
- **Permanence**: Every record becomes part of law enforcement's permanent database

### 2. **Permanent Location Tracking**

Every ALPR hit creates a precise, timestamped record of where you were and when. Over time, this data reveals intimate details of your life:

- Where you go for medical appointments
- Which religious institutions you attend
- Which neighborhoods you visit
- Your work commute patterns
- Your leisure activities and social connections
- Whether you're leaving a domestic abuse shelter

**This pattern of movement can be as revealing as reading someone's diary.**

### 3. **Indefinite Data Retention**

- **Default retention**: 30 days by many agencies
- **Extended retention**: Paying subscribers keep records for months or years
- **Some jurisdictions**: Store data indefinitely with no notification to residents
- **No deletion rights**: There is no mechanism to request your data be removed from ALPR databases

Data collected today may be searched through tomorrow in ways you cannot anticipate or prevent.

### 4. **Warrant-Free Access by Law Enforcement**

ALPR data is accessible to law enforcement without a warrant. Because license plates are visible on public roads, courts have consistently ruled that drivers have no expectation of privacy in their movements.

- **No judicial oversight**: Police can query databases without approval
- **Broad criteria**: Searches can use vague parameters (suspicious vehicle, person of interest, etc.)
- **Easy abuse**: The bar for access is extremely low, enabling political surveillance and harassment

### 5. **Unrestricted Sharing Across Jurisdictions**

Flock aggregates data across all subscribing agencies, creating a nationwide surveillance network:

- **Federal access**: ICE, FBI, and federal agencies can query local camera networks
- **No transparency**: Residents typically don't know their neighborhood is part of a broader network
- **Interstate coordination**: A camera in a quiet suburb feeds records to out-of-state law enforcement
- **Immigration enforcement**: ICE uses ALPR data to identify and track undocumented immigrants

### 6. **Chilling Effect on Civil Liberties**

Documented surveillance changes behavior. People adjust their routes and activities to avoid being tracked, which has real consequences:

- **Activists and journalists** avoid public assembly and organizing
- **Domestic abuse survivors** cannot safely travel to shelters or medical care
- **Protest attendance** carries permanent location records tied to your license plate
- **Minority communities** experience disproportionate surveillance and targeting
- **Religious freedom** is compromised when mosque, synagogue, and church attendance is tracked

### 7. **Staggering Error Rates**

ALPR systems generate false positives, leading to serious consequences:

- **Stolen vehicle errors**: Innocent drivers have been pulled over at gunpoint based on erroneous Flock alerts matching their plate to stolen vehicle databases
- **Expired data**: Plates remain flagged long after vehicles are recovered
- **Similar plates**: Misreadings occur, particularly with obscured or unusual plates
- **No accountability**: Victims have limited recourse when wrongly targeted

### 8. **Discriminatory Enforcement**

ALPR data amplifies existing police bias:

- **Targeted neighborhoods**: Law enforcement disproportionately monitors communities of color
- **Predictive harassment**: Drivers with certain profiles (race, location, vehicle type) face heightened scrutiny
- **Pretextual stops**: ALPR hits provide justification for traffic stops that enable racial profiling
- **Cumulative impact**: Every "routine" stop creates additional police interactions and records

### 9. **Data Misuse and Abuse**

Despite promises of limited use, ALPR data has been misused by:

- **Police officers** tracking ex-partners and personal acquaintances
- **Prying neighbors** using law enforcement access
- **Data brokers** who may purchase or access pooled surveillance data
- **Foreign governments** in some cases where data is shared internationally

### 10. **Normalization of Ubiquitous Surveillance**

Perhaps most dangerously, ALPR systems normalize a surveillance infrastructure that goes far beyond law enforcement:

- **Precedent setting**: Once accepted for cars, the same technology expands to facial recognition, gait analysis, and biometric tracking
- **Mission creep**: Systems deployed for "safety" expand to monitor political opponents and marginalized groups
- **Democratic erosion**: Pervasive surveillance fundamentally undermines freedom of movement and the right to anonymity

## The Lack of Meaningful Regulation

Unlike Europe's GDPR, the U.S. has no comprehensive federal privacy law addressing ALPR:

- **No consent requirement**: Citizens cannot opt out
- **No deletion rights**: You cannot request your data be erased
- **Limited transparency**: Many jurisdictions don't disclose they use ALPR
- **Weak oversight**: Most agencies have minimal internal controls on data access
- **No liability**: Companies and agencies face minimal consequences for misuse

## Who Is Most Vulnerable?

The dangers of ALPR fall disproportionately on:

- **Undocumented immigrants** tracked for deportation
- **Political activists** monitored for protest participation
- **Domestic abuse survivors** whose movements can be tracked by abusers with law enforcement access
- **Racial minorities** subject to discriminatory enforcement
- **Sex workers and LGBTQ+ individuals** vulnerable to exposure and harassment
- **Journalists** covering sensitive topics who need anonymity
- **People seeking sensitive medical care** (abortion, addiction treatment, mental health)

## What Can Be Done?

### Individual Level
- **Awareness**: Understand that your movements are being recorded
- **Advocacy**: Support organizations fighting surveillance expansion
- **Informed driving**: Be aware of ALPR camera locations in your community

### Community Level
- **Local pushback**: Organize against ALPR camera installation in your neighborhood
- **Transparency demands**: Request disclosure of ALPR use and data retention policies
- **Coalition building**: Join with other communities resisting surveillance expansion

### Policy Level
- **Federal legislation**: Demand ALPR regulation requiring warrants, data minimization, and strong privacy protections
- **Ballot initiatives**: Support ballot measures banning or restricting ALPR
- **Municipal action**: Push city councils to limit police access to ALPR data

## Conclusion

Automated License Plate Recognition represents a fundamental shift toward mass surveillance. Without meaningful legal restrictions, ALPR systems will continue expanding, creating permanent, searchable records of our movements accessible to law enforcement without warrants or judicial oversight.

The technology is not inherently evil—but in the current regulatory vacuum, it is being deployed as a tool of pervasive tracking that erodes privacy, enables discrimination, and fundamentally alters the freedom of movement that democracies depend on.

**Your movements should not require justification. They should not be recorded by default. They should not be accessible to every government agency with a query interface.**

The fight against ALPR is not about avoiding detection if you've done something wrong. It's about the fundamental right to move through the world without being cataloged, tracked, and permanently recorded by systems designed for mass surveillance rather than public safety.
