# Community ALPR Detection Guide: How to Find Automated License Plate Readers in Your Area

## Introduction

Automated License Plate Recognition (ALPR) cameras are becoming increasingly common in American communities, yet most residents remain unaware of their presence. This guide provides practical strategies for identifying ALPR camera locations, understanding their deployment patterns, and building community awareness about surveillance infrastructure.

## What to Look For: ALPR Camera Characteristics

### Physical Appearance

ALPR cameras typically differ from standard traffic or security cameras:

- **Size**: Usually mounted on poles or fixed structures; often larger than basic security cameras
- **Lens configuration**: May have multiple lenses pointing in different directions
- **Positioning**: Strategically placed to capture vehicle license plates clearly
- **Mounting height**: Typically 8-15 feet high, positioned at angles that face oncoming or passing traffic
- **Support equipment**: Often accompanied by additional hardware boxes (processing units, connectivity equipment)

### Common Flock Safety Indicators

Flock Safety cameras have specific identifiable features:

- **Distinctive housing**: Compact, weatherproof enclosures (roughly 12-18 inches)
- **Visible branding**: Sometimes labeled "FLOCK" on the unit itself
- **Dual-camera setup**: Often mounted with redundant cameras for reliability
- **Nighttime capability**: Equipped with infrared LEDs (visible as red glow at night)
- **Street light mounting**: Frequently attached to existing street lights

## Where ALPRs Are Typically Deployed

### High-Priority Surveillance Zones

**Highway on-ramps and exits**
- Monitors traffic entering/leaving major thoroughfares
- Captures commuters and traveling vehicles
- Often paired on both directions

**Toll plazas and toll-free roadways**
- Revenue generation and traffic enforcement
- May include signage or none at all
- Commonly found on interstate exits

**Neighborhood perimeters**
- Entrance/exit points to residential areas
- Bridges and major intersections surrounding communities
- Creates a "perimeter surveillance" effect

**Commercial districts and parking lots**
- Private ALPR systems monitoring retail areas
- Parking enforcement and towing coordination
- Often unmarked or indiscretely placed

**Police station parking lots**
- Inbound/outbound vehicle tracking
- May monitor arriving vehicles and license plates
- Sometimes visible during day shifts

**Court buildings and government centers**
- Tracks who visits legal proceedings
- Records courthouse visitors
- Often combined with facial recognition

**Border checkpoints and border towns**
- ICE coordination with local agencies
- Enhanced monitoring in communities near international borders

### Lower-Profile Deployment Sites

- **Fire stations** — Emergency responder tracking
- **Hospital parking lots** — Medical facility visitors
- **Schools and school bus routes** — Student transportation monitoring
- **Public parking garages** — Automated enforcement
- **Transit authority hubs** — Bus and train station perimeters
- **Shopping centers** — Cross-facility coordination with retail networks

## Detection Methods

### Method 1: Visual Community Survey

**What you'll need:**
- Walking shoes and a notebook/phone for documentation
- Binoculars for viewing pole-mounted equipment (optional)
- Camera or phone for photo documentation

**Systematic approach:**
1. Map your neighborhood's major entry/exit points
2. Walk or drive slowly through these areas, observing pole-mounted hardware
3. Note camera positioning, angle, and any identifying markings
4. Document GPS coordinates or street addresses
5. Take photos from multiple angles (helpful for comparison)
6. Look for paired cameras (one per direction)

**Areas to focus on:**
- Major intersections
- Highway on/off ramps
- Neighborhood entrance/exit roads
- Streets leading to government buildings
- Main commercial thoroughfares

### Method 2: Research Public Records and FOIA Requests

**Online resources:**
- **City council meeting minutes** — ALPR deployments are sometimes discussed
- **Police department websites** — Some publish ALPR location information
- **County assessor records** — May list camera infrastructure
- **Budget documents** — Police budgets often itemize camera purchases/maintenance
- **News archives** — Local reporting on camera installations

**FOIA Requests to file:**

Submit formal Freedom of Information Act requests to:
- Local police department
- County sheriff's office
- City public safety department

**Sample request template:**

> "I request all records related to Automated License Plate Recognition (ALPR) cameras deployed by [Agency Name], including: (1) location coordinates for all ALPR cameras, (2) dates of installation, (3) vendor information (e.g., Flock Safety), (4) data retention policies, (5) access logs showing which agencies have queried ALPR data, and (6) policies governing use of ALPR data. Please provide in digital format."

**Follow-up options:**
- Request annual maintenance records
- Ask for data access audit logs
- Inquire about inter-agency data sharing agreements
- Request retention and deletion policies

### Method 3: Crowdsourced Mapping Platforms

**Existing surveillance databases:**

- **Atlas of Surveillance** (eff.org/atlas) — EFF's comprehensive tracking of surveillance systems
- **SurveillanceMap.org** — Community-contributed camera locations
- **OpenStreetMap** — Some communities add ALPR locations as tags
- **Reddit communities** — r/privacy and r/surveillance sometimes discuss local deployments

**Creating local documentation:**

- Start a shared spreadsheet (Google Maps or similar)
- Document addresses, camera types, installation dates
- Share with local advocacy groups
- Build a neighborhood ALPR inventory

### Method 4: Technical Detection

**For technically-inclined community members:**

**Radio frequency detection:**
- ALPR cameras emit radio signals for data transmission
- Software-defined radio (SDR) enthusiasts can detect these frequencies
- Legal in most jurisdictions for passive monitoring
- Requires specific equipment ($20-300 range)

**Network reconnaissance:**
- Some municipalities publish public camera API endpoints
- Traffic camera live feeds may expose ALPR infrastructure
- Use tools like Shodan.io to search for exposed camera systems
- Legal only if you don't access restricted systems

**Data analysis:**
- Analyze police activity reports for location patterns
- Cross-reference high-ticket-writing areas with ALPR installations
- Look for traffic citations issued in clusters (indicator of enforcement infrastructure)

### Method 5: Community Informants and Insider Information

**Build relationships with:**
- **City council members** — Often have detailed infrastructure maps
- **Neighborhood association leaders** — May have detailed deployment knowledge
- **Local journalists** — Often track surveillance expansion
- **City planners** — Have records of infrastructure permits
- **Public works employees** — May be aware of camera installations

**Information requests to make:**

- "Has our neighborhood received ALPR cameras?"
- "How many ALPR systems does the city operate?"
- "What is the annual maintenance cost for surveillance?"
- "Which agencies have access to ALPR data?"

## Safety Considerations

### Legal Protections

- **You have the right** to observe public infrastructure and take photos of cameras
- **FOIA requests are protected** — Agencies cannot retaliate for requesting public records
- **First Amendment protects** advocacy and organizing around surveillance
- **Be cautious** in areas where police are known to harass photographers

### Personal Safety

- Document camera locations during daylight when possible
- Travel with others for community mapping activities
- Inform others of your location and plans
- Do not trespass on private property to access camera equipment
- Be aware of police presence in high-surveillance areas

## What NOT to Do

- **Do not physically interfere** with camera equipment
- **Do not trespass** to get closer views or access cables
- **Do not hack** into camera systems or networks
- **Do not damage** infrastructure (this is illegal)
- **Do not impersonate** police or city officials
- **Do not film** police without understanding your jurisdiction's laws

(Legal documentation and technical investigation must stay within bounds of law)

## Recognizing Patterns: Analysis Tips

### Surveillance Clustering

Look for these patterns:

- **Perimeter surveillance** — Cameras concentrated around neighborhood boundaries
- **Border monitoring** — Heavy deployment near immigrant communities
- **Route tracking** — Cameras along specific travel corridors (highways, main streets)
- **Destination mapping** — Cameras at common gathering places (mosques, protests sites, community centers)

### Correlation with Enforcement

- Compare ALPR locations with police citation data
- Identify if high-ticket areas align with camera locations
- Analyze whether specific communities are disproportionately surveilled
- Track changes in enforcement patterns after ALPR installation

### Temporal Patterns

- **Installation dates** — Are cameras installed in phases?
- **Seasonal changes** — Do police increase ALPR queries during certain times?
- **Policy shifts** — Does new police leadership expand/limit ALPR use?

## Reporting to DeFlock: Crowdsourced Surveillance Tracking

DeFlock is a community-driven platform designed to map and track Automated License Plate Recognition (ALPR) camera deployments, particularly Flock Safety systems. By reporting camera locations to DeFlock, you contribute to a public database that increases transparency about surveillance infrastructure and empowers communities with information.

### What is DeFlock?

DeFlock (Defeat Flock) is a collaborative mapping project that:

- **Aggregates ALPR locations** — Collects reports from community members about camera sightings
- **Creates public visibility** — Makes surveillance infrastructure transparent and documented
- **Tracks patterns** — Identifies surveillance clustering and disparate deployment across communities
- **Supports advocacy** — Provides data for policy campaigns and legal challenges
- **Maintains privacy** — Protects reporter anonymity while verifying submissions

### Why Report to DeFlock?

**Benefits of participation:**

1. **Build public awareness** — Transform hidden surveillance into documented reality
2. **Support research** — Academic researchers use DeFlock data to study surveillance disparities
3. **Enable advocacy** — Local organizations use DeFlock maps in policy campaigns
4. **Legal documentation** — Creates evidence for constitutional challenges to ALPR programs
5. **Community organizing** — Helps residents in other neighborhoods find local surveillance
6. **Accountability** — Makes Flock Safety's footprint undeniable and measurable

### How to Report to DeFlock

#### Step 1: Gather Required Information

Before submitting a report, collect:

- **Location address or intersection** — Be as specific as possible
- **GPS coordinates** — Use your phone's built-in GPS or Google Maps
- **Camera type** — Is it a Flock Safety camera? Other vendor? Unknown?
- **Camera positioning** — Which direction does it face? How high is it mounted?
- **Installation date** — If known (approximate is acceptable)
- **Photo evidence** — Optional but highly valuable for verification
- **Supporting details**:
  - Is it mounted on a pole, building, street light, or other structure?
  - Are there multiple cameras in the area?
  - What nearby businesses or facilities are present?
  - Is there signage indicating surveillance?

#### Step 2: Access the DeFlock Platform

**Online:**
- Visit **deflock.org** (or current DeFlock mapping platform)
- Create a free account (or report anonymously if the platform allows)
- Locate your state/municipality on the map

**Alternative platforms (if DeFlock is unavailable):**
- **Atlas of Surveillance** (eff.org/atlas) — EFF's official ALPR tracker
- **SurveillanceMap.org** — Community surveillance database
- Email privacy advocacy groups with your sighting

#### Step 3: Submit Your Report

**Information to include in your submission:**

**Basic Details:**
```
Location: [Specific address or intersection]
Coordinates: [Latitude, Longitude]
Camera Type: [Flock Safety / Unknown / Other vendor]
Date of Sighting: [MM/DD/YYYY]
Direction of Coverage: [North / South / East / West / Bidirectional]
```

**Description:**
```
"Flock Safety camera mounted on street light at [address]. Camera 
faces [direction], approximately [height] feet high. Installed 
approximately [date/timeframe]. Located near [nearby landmarks]."
```

**Photo Submission:**
- Upload clear photos showing:
  - Full camera and mounting structure
  - Camera positioning and direction
  - Street context (intersections, buildings, landmarks)
- Photos should clearly show the camera (zoomed in if possible)
- Multiple angles strengthen verification

**Additional Context (optional but helpful):**
- Nearby police station or city infrastructure
- Whether the area is residential, commercial, or mixed
- Demographic information about the neighborhood
- Any visible signage or official markings
- Whether other surveillance cameras are nearby

#### Step 4: Verification and Publication

DeFlock's verification process:

1. **Initial review** — Staff or volunteer moderators review submissions
2. **Fact-checking** — Cross-reference with public records and other reports
3. **Photo verification** — Confirm camera type and location from images
4. **Publication** — Approved reports are added to the public map
5. **Community feedback** — Other users may confirm or provide additional details

**What happens to your report:**
- It becomes part of the public record
- Your anonymity is protected (if requested)
- The location is marked on community maps
- Other residents can view and verify the sighting
- Advocacy groups can use it for policy work

### Tips for Strong DeFlock Reports

**Make your report more valuable:**

1. **Be specific** — "Corner of Main and Oak" is better than "somewhere downtown"
2. **Provide photos** — Image evidence dramatically increases credibility
3. **Include context** — Nearby schools, police stations, or community centers matter
4. **Add details** — Mounting type, height, direction, and supporting infrastructure
5. **Update regularly** — If you observe changes (new cameras, removals, modifications)
6. **Cross-reference** — Mention if you've filed FOIA requests about the camera
7. **Document patterns** — Report if multiple cameras cluster in one area

### What DeFlock Data Shows

Submitted reports reveal:

- **Surveillance concentration** — Which neighborhoods are most heavily monitored
- **Perimeter policing** — Cameras clustering around specific communities
- **Vendor patterns** — Which companies profit from local surveillance
- **Equity disparities** — Whether surveillance correlates with race/income
- **Expansion trends** — How rapidly ALPR networks are growing

### Protecting Your Privacy While Reporting

**Safety measures:**

1. **Anonymous submission** — Most platforms allow anonymous reports
2. **Use VPN** — Access DeFlock or submission platforms through a VPN if concerned
3. **Separate account** — Use a dedicated email not linked to your primary identity
4. **Avoid identifying details** — Don't include information that could identify you
5. **Report in groups** — Submit reports as part of community mapping walks (less individually traceable)

**Note:** Reporting camera locations is legal and protected speech. You have the right to document public infrastructure without fear of retaliation.

### Challenges and Limitations

**Be aware of DeFlock's limitations:**

- **Verification delays** — It may take time for reports to be reviewed and published
- **Map incompleteness** — Private ALPR systems may not be documented
- **Data privacy** — Some details (exact camera angles, technical specs) may be redacted
- **Geographic variation** — Underreporting in some communities, overreporting in others
- **Platform stability** — Crowdsourced platforms may have limited resources

**Mitigating limitations:**

- Report to multiple platforms (DeFlock, Atlas of Surveillance, etc.)
- File FOIA requests to confirm official deployments
- Organize local groups to ensure comprehensive coverage
- Maintain independent documentation in case platforms change
- Share findings with advocacy organizations for cross-verification

## Conclusion

ALPR cameras are increasingly ubiquitous, but they remain largely invisible to the communities they monitor. By systematically documenting their locations and sharing findings with neighbors and policymakers, communities can begin to understand the full scope of surveillance infrastructure and organize for change.
